Write Your New York: The Heart Where We Meet



I vividly remember visiting a neighborhood in Brooklyn every Saturday with my dad to visit my cousins and their family. This neighborhood is uniquely divided by Eighth Avenue: on the west side is a thriving and large Hispanic community, while on the east is a predominantly Asian community. Despite this division, both cultures unite within the heart of this neighborhood: the park. It's a shared space where the two cultures mix and create unity. This neighborhood is called Sunset Park. I remember when I first arrived at the west side of Sunset Park. The first thing that caught my eye was the large amount of Hispanics in the street every morning, especially Mexicans. The streets were lined with bustling Hispanic bakeries, markets, eloteros, and carts selling tamales, with many Hispanics walking around the street hoping to buy some tamales with some Mexican bread early in the morning before they sold out. I was one of those Mexicans, every time I came here I would wander the streets, hoping to buy something before going into the park to meet my family. That being said, when you walk into the actual Park, the neighborhood's diversity becomes even clearer as you see both Asians and Hispanics passing time here. Every day without fail, you find a group of Asian aunties dancing to the rhythm of music playing from a small radio, while Asian uncles sit by the tables, smoking and playing chess or mahjong, meanwhile on the soccer field and basketball courts, Hispanic men were zeroed in on their games. Away from all this, in the open fields you see people relaxing, just enjoying their day. This is how it is, the cultures here co-exist, a place where everyone comes to live their life.

Although Sunset Park is a place everyone including myself knows for its cultural diversity, Recently, Sunset Park has been changing, for the worse. Sunset Park’s cultural diversity has been fading throughout the years because the neighborhood is directly confronted with gentrification pressures. This is something we can’t overlook, especially when the wealthier have been pushing out the long-time residents who have always been here, Changing the genuineness of Sunset Park. Not only have they been pushing out the people, but now if you walk through Sunset Park, the small family-owned Hispanic and Asian stores you once saw daily are replaced by high-end cafes and restaurants. Ever since I first saw this gentrification happen firsthand, the family-owned stores I used to go to every weekend with my dad are just a memory, now, they are just chain stores with no cultural significance to Sunset Park. Even through all this, Sunset Park is still standing and is resisting all this change. Each passing day the residents of Sunset Park continue to meet at the park to dance to the same music from that small radio, play games of chess and smoke, and play the same sports. This makes Sunset Park, Sunset Park. It thrives because the people here have such a strong connection to the park, setting Sunset Park apart from any other neighborhood in New York.

Unlike the other neighborhoods in New York, the park itself, is what makes this neighborhood unique. It serves as a second home for people to express themselves and their cultures, even though everyone is so different from each other. Mixing these two cultures into one creates a community different from others. Sometimes when you’re walking on the street you see an Asian grocery store next to a Hispanic bakery on the east side of Eighth Avenue and you often see a Hispanic man on the east side of Sunset Park that is supposed to mainly have Asians and an Asian man in the west side that is supposed to mainly have Hispanics. Despite the differences in culture and identity they still mix. Even inside the park, the cultures often mix, with the Chinese New Year parades and celebrations and the Hispanic celebrations and holidays. This slight overlap in cultures, greatly impacts the community, more so the newer generations born into this cultural mix. You’ll see the playground and the Hispanic and Asian kids playing, you’ll see the Hispanic and Asian kids playing tag in the middle of the roads, alongside the kids playing basketball. Sunset Park truly separates the divide between the two cultures, creating a bridge between them, and allowing them to mix freely.
